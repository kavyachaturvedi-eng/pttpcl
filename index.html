02 Problem Statement

Three structural problems block operational AI from delivering real outcomes.

These aren't technology gaps. They are operational realities that any AI system — custom or platform — must solve before it earns engineer trust and measurable results.

Problem 01

Machinery malfunctions are caught too late — reactively, manually, expensively.

How it works today

When a machine starts degrading, the typical sequence is: operator notices something → engineer dispatched → opens 5 systems manually (PI, SAP, SharePoint, P&ID drawings, OEM manuals) → cross-references by hand → forms a hypothesis → calls a senior engineer to validate → recommends action. This takes 4–8 hours. Often days. 52% of refinery shutdowns are unplanned and reactive.

Operational impact

Each unplanned shutdown on a CDU-class unit requires mobilising maintenance crews, sourcing parts, and managing the downstream production cascade — all under pressure, at any hour. The cost is not just downtime. It is the erosion of operational confidence and the accumulation of deferred risk.

Problem 02

60–70% of engineer time is spent gathering data, not analysing it.

How it works today

Sensor data lives in OSIsoft PI. Maintenance records sit in SAP PM. P&IDs hide in CAD systems. Engineering notes pile up in SharePoint. Decades of tag renames, sensor swaps, and inconsistent asset numbering across refineries have eroded data quality — without anyone building a unified view.

Operational impact

Senior reliability engineers — the people who actually know the plant — spend the majority of their shift gathering context rather than applying judgment. This is not a tool problem. It is a data fragmentation problem. And it means that even experienced engineers are operating with an incomplete picture.

Problem 03

Senior engineers are retiring — and their pattern recognition is leaving with them.

How it works today

The engineers who know that "P-204 runs hot in monsoon season — always check lubricant first, bearing second" carry that knowledge in their heads. It is not documented. It is not searchable. When they retire, it is gone. New engineers on night shifts make decisions without the benefit of 20 years of pattern recognition from the same unit.

Operational impact

Institutional knowledge loss is the slowest, least visible, and most structurally damaging operational risk in mature refineries. It shows up not as a single incident but as a gradual erosion of the organisation's ability to diagnose and resolve complex failures quickly — the exact capability Mission X depends on.

Why AI pilots have stalled before

The root cause is not AI quality — it is data fragmentation.

Generic AI platforms applied to fragmented industrial data hallucinate, drift, and lose engineer trust within weeks. The models are not the problem. The missing step is a purpose-built industrial data foundation — a model that connects your sensor tags to your assets, your maintenance history, your P&IDs, and your engineers' knowledge — before a single agent is trained on it. That foundation must come first, and it must be fitted to your operational reality, not a vendor template.
